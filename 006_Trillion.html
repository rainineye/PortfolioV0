// Project Background

Trillion is a chain-agnostic money market built on Entangle's interoperable infrastructure. It allows users to supply assets for passive yield or borrow against their collateral across multiple blockchain networks through a single, unified interface. As the protocol expanded from simple supply staking into borrowing and Supercharged (leveraged) strategies, the product needed an interface that could support varying levels of user proficiency and risk appetite while staying clear, intuitive, and consistent within an inherently omnichain environment.

// Target Users

Passive Earn Users
(Novice to Intermediate)

Users who primarily supply assets for yield, prefer low-risk strategies, and need a clear, simplified interface without dealing with liquidation.

Advanced DeFi Users
(Borrowing & Leverage Strategies)

Experienced users who actively borrow, manage collateral, and utilize leveraged positions (Supercharge). They require detailed metrics, transparent PnL, risk indicators, and pro-level controls.

1. New Information Architecture

When I joined Trillion, the protocol had already gone through several design iterations. My first step was to reorganize the information architecture around user goals and the roadmap of upcoming features. While V0 primarily supported staking-based supply, it was clear that once borrowing and Supercharge (leveraged positions) were enabled in V1, the interface would need to accommodate users with very different risk profiles and levels of proficiency.

2.Toggling Modes

To manage this spectrum, I introduced a mode toggle that separates a simplified view for novice users from a pro setup for advanced users: As novice users see only the Earn opportunities staying spot, pro users gain full access to leverage-specific features upon a series of tutorials and knowledge check for liquidation risks.

// Problem Statement

At the time, most DeFi protocols showed supplied positions "as is," mixing yield and price effects in a way that made real profitability hard to understand. The challenge was to separate these signals, reduce cognitive load, and maintain cross-chain clarity while surfacing only the metrics that genuinely inform decisions. In short, the interface needed to give users clear, contextual, and transparent performance insights without requiring further calculation or guesswork.

1. Decision-Ready Pool Cards

Each pool card surfaces the metrics that matter most to user decision-making: net APR, borrowing utilization (as a proxy for withdrawal risk), native TVL, and incentive rewards.

Network information is available when relevant to reinforce Trillion's chain-agnostic experience.

4. Position cards with USD/Token-denominated PnL, list/card view

2. Performance-Focused Position Cards

For positions, the two variables that truly impact performance are price movement and yield generation. In many existing protocols, real PnL is difficult to track—whether intentionally or not—which forces users to calculate profitability outside the interface. Improving this transparency became a central UX priority.

Drawing inspiration from CEX perpetuals, I redesigned position displays to place net PnL at the highest visual hierarchy, supported by expanded yield-specific metrics.

A toggle allows users to switch between token-denominated and USD-denominated views, giving both native and dollar-based users clarity on performance. To support different trading mental models, Trillion provides both card view (DEX-like) and list view (CEX-like) layouts.

3. Key flows & feature UX in Lo-fi

1. Staking Flow: Approve → Stake → Staking → Success + Show Balance (V0)

2. Manage Positions: Supply / Withdraw → Approve → Supplying / Withdrawing → Success + Realized PnL (V0)

3. Add Liquidity: 1:1, Zap or with Custom Range (V1)

4. Wallet dropdown: Multi-asset Management (V1)

4. Conceptual Feature Proposal

For Supercharged positions, I proposed introducing TP/SL (Take Profit / Stop Loss) automation - another UX aligned to CEX catered to traders. In leveraged farming, cascading liquidations often occur in a few blocks and it is nearly impossible for users to exit manually, making automated protection especially valuable.

The concept was well-received by engineering and leadership and added to the product backlog.
